Neurology

Neurology is the branch of medicine that studies the nervous system, including the brain, spinal cord, peripheral nerves, and neuromuscular junctions. It focuses on neural signaling, cognition, sensation, movement, and neurological disorders such as stroke, epilepsy, Parkinson’s disease, Alzheimer’s disease, and multiple sclerosis.

🧠 Brain Anatomy

The brain is the control center of the human body, responsible for cognition, movement, sensation, emotion, and autonomic regulation.

📍 Cerebral Lobes

Different brain regions perform specialized functions.

Frontal Lobe

Executive function, voluntary movement, personality, speech production.

Parietal Lobe

Sensory integration, touch perception, spatial orientation.

Temporal Lobe

Hearing, language comprehension, memory formation.

Occipital Lobe

Visual processing and interpretation.

⚡ Major Neural Pathways

High-yield ascending and descending neurological tracts.

Corticospinal Tract

Controls voluntary motor movement. Damage causes weakness and spasticity.

Spinothalamic Tract

Carries pain and temperature sensation.

Dorsal Column

Carries proprioception, vibration, and fine touch sensation.

🩺 Common Neurological Disorders

Major diseases affecting the nervous system.

Stroke

Interruption of cerebral blood flow causing focal neurological deficits.

Parkinson’s Disease

Degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra.

Epilepsy

Abnormal excessive neuronal activity causing recurrent seizures.

Multiple Sclerosis

Autoimmune demyelinating disorder affecting CNS pathways.

📋 Clinical Case Study

Applying neuroanatomical localization clinically.

A 65-year-old presents with sudden right-sided weakness, facial droop, and slurred speech.


Likely Diagnosis: Left-sided ischemic stroke


Key Concept: Brain lesions commonly produce contralateral neurological deficits.


Immediate Action: Urgent CT imaging and stroke evaluation.

💡 Clinical Tips

High-yield neurological examination concepts.

FAST = Face, Arm, Speech, Time for stroke recognition.
Always localize the lesion before diagnosing.
Differentiate UMN from LMN lesions carefully.
Neuroanatomy becomes easier when linked clinically.
